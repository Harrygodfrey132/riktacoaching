      <aside class="adhd-screening__note">
        <h3>Instructions for clinicians</h3>
        <p><strong>Symptoms:</strong> Score Part A. If four or more marks appear in the darkly shaded boxes within Part A, then the patient has symptoms highly consistent with ADHD in adults and further investigation is warranted.</p>
        <p><strong>Part B:</strong> The frequency scores provide additional cues. Pay particular attention to marks appearing in the dark shaded boxes. No total score or diagnostic likelihood is utilized for the twelve questions.</p>
        <p><strong>Impairments:</strong> Review the entire checklist and evaluate impairment across work/school, social, and family settings.</p>
        <p><strong>History:</strong> Assess symptom presence in childhood. Some significant symptoms should have been present in childhood, but full symptomology is not necessary.</p>
        <p><strong>Note:</strong> This checklist supports clinical discussion and does not replace a professional diagnosis.</p>

        <h2>The Value of Screening for Adults With ADHD</h2>
        <p>Research suggests that ADHD symptoms can persist into adulthood, impacting relationships, careers, and personal safety. Because ADHD is often misunderstood, many people do not receive appropriate treatment. The ASRS v1.1 can help identify adults who may need a more in-depth clinical interview.</p>
        <p>The ASRS v1.1 was developed in conjunction with the World Health Organization (WHO) and a workgroup on Adult ADHD.</p>
