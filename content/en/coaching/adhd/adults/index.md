<section class="coachning-section" id="adult-adhd-pressure">
  <div class="coachning-panel">
    <h2>Why ADHD often feels different in adulthood</h2>
    <p>
      As an adult, the problem is rarely just concentration. It is the accumulation of invisible demands. You are expected to prioritise work, remember appointments, manage finances, reply to people, keep a home running, and stay emotionally steady while doing it. ADHD turns those background demands into a constant source of drag, even when you are capable and trying hard.
    </p>
    <ul class="coachning-list">
      <li><strong>Workload spillover:</strong> Tasks that should take one hour can follow you around mentally all day.</li>
      <li><strong>Admin avoidance:</strong> Bills, forms, bookings, and inboxes become harder the longer they sit.</li>
      <li><strong>Unstable routines:</strong> Good intentions collapse quickly when life gets busy or energy drops.</li>
      <li><strong>Relationship strain:</strong> Forgetting, lateness, or inconsistent follow-through can create tension at home.</li>
      <li><strong>Burnout cycles:</strong> You compensate hard, keep pushing, then crash and lose momentum.</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="coachning-section" id="adult-coaching-focus">
  <div class="coachning-panel">
    <h2>What adult ADHD coaching actually helps with</h2>
    <p>
      Adult coaching is not about trying harder or becoming a different person. It is about identifying where your current setup breaks down and replacing it with something simpler, clearer, and easier to repeat when life is full.
    </p>
    <ul class="coachning-list">
      <li><strong>Planning the week:</strong> Stop holding everything in your head and build a working system for priorities, time blocks, and recovery.</li>
      <li><strong>Task initiation:</strong> Reduce the delay between knowing what matters and actually starting.</li>
      <li><strong>Work performance:</strong> Improve transitions, meeting preparation, task tracking, and completion without relying on last-minute panic.</li>
      <li><strong>Home and life admin:</strong> Create repeatable routines for bills, appointments, household tasks, and messages.</li>
      <li><strong>Emotional regulation:</strong> Work on irritability, shame, overwhelm, and the mental crash that can follow overcompensating.</li>
      <li><strong>Relationships:</strong> Build better communication around responsibility, lateness, forgetting, and unmet expectations.</li>
      <li><strong>Burnout prevention:</strong> Spot the patterns that keep pushing you into boom-and-bust cycles.</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="coachning-section" id="how-adult-coaching-works">
  <div class="coachning-panel">
    <h2>How adult ADHD coaching works in practice</h2>
    <p>
      We start by working out where the pressure actually lands in your life. For some people it is work execution. For others it is home admin, emotional regulation, or constantly overcommitting and then collapsing. From there, coaching becomes practical and specific. We look at what is breaking, what you are compensating for, and what can be simplified.
    </p>
    <div class="program-modules">
      <h4>Typical areas of work in coaching</h4>
      <ol class="module-list">
        <li>
          <span class="module-number">1</span>
          <div>
            <h5>Map the actual friction</h5>
            <p>Identify where missed deadlines, avoidance, emotional overload, or home chaos are starting.</p>
          </div>
        </li>
        <li>
          <span class="module-number">2</span>
          <div>
            <h5>Build a usable weekly structure</h5>
            <p>Create routines for priorities, admin, and recovery that can survive a busy week.</p>
          </div>
        </li>
        <li>
          <span class="module-number">3</span>
          <div>
            <h5>Improve initiation and completion</h5>
            <p>Use repeatable methods to start faster, finish more, and rely less on panic.</p>

        </li>
        <li>
          <span class="module-number">4</span>
          <div>
            <h5>Reduce shame-driven overcompensation</h5>
